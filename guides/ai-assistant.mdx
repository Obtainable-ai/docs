The PeerNotes AI assistant is designed around a simple belief: the best way to help you think is to ask better questions, not to hand you someone else's words. Instead of generating content for you, the assistant reads what you've written and pushes back — raising the gaps, weak spots, and questions your readers will ask — so you leave with a sharper, more considered version of your own thinking.

<Note>
  The AI assistant is currently on the roadmap and is not yet available in PeerNotes. This page describes what to expect when it ships.
</Note>

## Philosophy: a thinking partner, not a ghostwriter

Most AI writing tools offer to write things for you. The PeerNotes AI assistant takes the opposite approach. The goal isn't to produce text — it's to improve yours.

When you share a note with the assistant, it acts as a critical reader: the kind of colleague who reads your draft carefully and comes back with the questions you hadn't thought to answer yet. It pressure-tests your reasoning, surfaces assumptions you've left implicit, and flags places where your argument or idea needs more support.

The result is thinking that's genuinely yours — just sharper than it would have been without a second perspective.

## How it works

<Steps>
  <Step title="Open a note you want to develop">
    The AI assistant works from notes you've already written. Start by getting your thinking down — even a rough draft gives the assistant enough to work with.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Invite the assistant to respond">
    With a note open, select **Ask AI assistant** from the note toolbar. The assistant reads your note in full before responding.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Review the assistant's questions">
    The assistant returns a set of questions and observations — not a rewrite. These might include: questions your audience will likely ask, claims that need more evidence, assumptions worth making explicit, or angles you haven't considered.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Revise your note">
    Take the questions that resonate, go back into your note, and address them in your own words. You decide what's useful and what to set aside.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Tip>
  The assistant is most useful on notes where you have a real point to make but aren't yet sure it holds up. Use it on drafts you'd want a thoughtful colleague to read before you publish to the team.
</Tip>

## What to expect when the assistant ships

When the AI assistant becomes available, it will be accessible directly from the note editor — no separate interface to navigate to. Here's what the initial release will include:

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Critical questions" icon="circle-question">
    The assistant surfaces the questions a careful reader would ask — the ones you need to answer before your note is as strong as it can be.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Assumption spotting" icon="magnifying-glass">
    It flags claims and premises in your note that are doing a lot of work without much support, so you can decide whether to back them up or rethink them.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Audience perspective" icon="users">
    The assistant considers how your teammates are likely to read the note — not just whether the argument is sound in the abstract, but whether it will land.
  </Card>
  <Card title="On-demand feedback" icon="comment">
    You invite the assistant in when you want it. It doesn't monitor your notes or offer unsolicited feedback while you're writing.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

## The long-term vision

Over time, the PeerNotes AI assistant is designed to become more attuned to the way your specific team thinks and communicates. As it learns from the notes and discussions in your workspace, it will develop a sense of:

- **Your team's vocabulary** — the terms, shorthand, and concepts that are specific to your work
- **Your standards for a good argument** — what counts as sufficient evidence or a well-supported claim in your domain
- **Recurring themes and open questions** — patterns across your workspace that it can connect to new notes you're developing

The goal is an assistant that feels like a teammate who has read everything your team has ever written — and can bring that context to bear when you ask for a second opinion.
