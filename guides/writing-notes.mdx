Notes are the core unit of thinking in PeerNotes. Every idea, draft, observation, or decision your team wants to develop lives as a note — a title and a body that anyone on the team can read, build on, and refine together. Writing a note is the starting point for turning a rough thought into something your whole team can work with.

## Create a new note

<Steps>
  <Step title="Open your workspace">
    Go to [peernotes.io](https://peernotes.io) and sign in. You'll land directly in your shared workspace.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Start a new note">
    Click **New note** in the toolbar or sidebar. A blank note opens in the editor with the title field ready to type.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Add a title">
    Give your note a clear, specific title. The title is what your teammates see in the workspace feed, so make it descriptive enough that they know what the note is about at a glance. For example: *"Onboarding flow problems we need to fix"* is more useful than *"Onboarding thoughts"*.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Write your note body">
    Click into the body and start writing. The editor supports plain prose as well as formatting — headers, bullet lists, numbered lists, and bold or italic text. Write as much or as little as you need; a note can be a single sentence or several detailed paragraphs.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Organize notes with topics

Topics work like lightweight categories that keep your workspace navigable as it grows. You can add one or more topics to any note.

<Info>
  To add a topic, open the note and look for the **Topics** field below the title. Type a topic name and press Enter. If the topic already exists in your workspace, it will appear as a suggestion as you type — select it to link the note to the existing topic rather than creating a duplicate.
</Info>

Using consistent topic names across your team makes it easy to find all the notes related to a particular project, question, or area of work. Agree on a short list of core topics with your team early on to keep things organized.

## Edit and format notes

PeerNotes uses a straightforward editor focused on prose and structure — not complex document formatting. Here's what's available:

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Headers" icon="heading">
    Use headers to break a longer note into sections. They make a note easier to scan and signal structure to your teammates.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Lists" icon="list">
    Bullet lists work well for observations, options, and open questions. Numbered lists are useful for steps or ranked ideas.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Bold and italic" icon="bold">
    Emphasize key terms or important points. Use sparingly — too much emphasis means nothing stands out.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Links" icon="link">
    Paste a URL and PeerNotes turns it into a clickable link. Useful for referencing source material or related workspace content.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

To edit an existing note, open it and click anywhere in the title or body to start making changes. Your edits save automatically as you type.

## Save and publish to the shared workspace

Notes save automatically as you write — you won't lose work if you close the tab or switch to another note. When you're ready for your teammates to see a note, publish it to the shared workspace.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Finish your draft">
    Write and format your note to a point where it's useful for others — it doesn't need to be perfect, but it should be coherent.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Publish the note">
    Click **Publish** in the top-right corner of the editor. Your note becomes visible to everyone in your workspace immediately.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Notify your team (optional)">
    After publishing, you can mention specific teammates in the note to draw their attention to it.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Note>
  Unpublished notes are only visible to you. Publish a note whenever you want your team to read, respond to, or build on your thinking.
</Note>

## Tips for writing notes your team can actually use

Good notes for collaboration are a little different from notes you write just for yourself. A few practices that make a difference:

<Tip>
  **Lead with your point.** Put the most important thing — your conclusion, your question, your recommendation — in the first sentence. Teammates skimming the workspace feed will read that first line and decide whether to open the note.
</Tip>

<Tip>
  **Write for a reader who wasn't there.** If your note references a meeting, a conversation, or context your team might not have, add a sentence or two of background. Notes that assume shared context age badly.
</Tip>

<Tip>
  **Keep one idea per note.** Resist the urge to bundle multiple topics into one note. Focused notes are easier to discuss, easier to find later, and easier to build on.
</Tip>

<Tip>
  **Use questions as titles.** Notes titled as questions — *"Should we change our onboarding sequence?"* — invite responses and make it clear the note is inviting input, not announcing a decision.
</Tip>
