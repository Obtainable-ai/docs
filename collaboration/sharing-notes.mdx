Sharing in PeerNotes happens in two deliberate stages. First you **share a note** with workspace members to get feedback while the thinking is still developing. Then, when the note holds up, you **publish it as an article** — the finished version, for the whole workspace. Keeping these separate is the point: feedback happens early, publication means something.

## Stage 1: Share a note for feedback

<Steps>
  <Step title="Open the note's share panel">
    From the note editor, open the share panel to control who can see the note.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Choose your reviewers">
    Share with your whole workspace or with specific members — useful for early drafts, sensitive topics, or when you want a couple of trusted readers before going wider.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Invite challenge">
    Say what kind of input you want. "Draft — poke holes in the argument" gets you better feedback than an unlabelled document ever will.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Info>
  You can adjust a note's sharing at any time — widen it as the draft matures, or keep it narrow while the idea is fragile.
</Info>

## Stage 2: Publish as an article

When review has done its work, publish:

<Steps>
  <Step title="Select Publish in the note editor">
    The publish dialog previews the article exactly as readers will see it — title, excerpt, and estimated reading time.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Choose note visibility">
    Decide whether readers can see the working note behind the article. Keep it visible to let teammates trace how the thinking developed; hide it to keep focus on the finished piece.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Publish">
    The article appears in the workspace's [Articles](/core-concepts/articles) feed for every member.
  </Step>
</Steps>

If the thinking evolves later, edit the note and **republish** — the article updates in place.

## What stays private

Your [thoughts](/core-concepts/thoughts) and [sources](/core-concepts/sources) are always private to you. Teammates only ever see what you deliberately share: notes you've opened to them, and articles you've published. That privacy is what makes honest capture possible — you can think freely, then choose what's ready for other eyes.

<Warning>
  Avoid publishing a work-in-progress as an article. Articles carry a quality signal for your teammates — for early feedback, share the note instead.
</Warning>
